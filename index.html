The premise
Human judgement is the core – AI is the support, at every step.
Human judgement – the through-line AI – support, at every step

That's the premise everything here builds on – and it raises the real question: does our use of AI strengthen that thread, or quietly replace it?

Observed in practice
Students are handing their thinking to AI – and it's the higher‑order thinking they hand over most.

Higher-order is the deep end of learning – analysing, evaluating, creating.

And it's exactly where students use AI most: in real usage, creating 39.8% and analysing 30.2% top the list – routine tasks sit near 10% each. (Anthropic student-usage data · Stanford AI Index 2026)

Which is the exact judgement our assessments are built to grow.
The shift
So the question isn't whether to allow AI. It's whether AI can push them to think harder.

Coaching

It questions your reasoning, tests your argument, and helps you build one that's genuinely yours – and it tells you the truth when an idea is generic, instead of agreeing.

Guardrail it won't write it, find the sources, or hand over the answer – that part stays the student's, and the line doesn't move.

Soft words, hard actions – the conversation stays warm; the boundary never bends.

It starts from respect – students are adults who'll use AI either way, so good habits are built inside the work itself. Explicit, tested boundaries give the teaching team confidence too.

A method, not a tool. Generic by default, or tailored to a specific assessment – a single plain-text file students paste into any AI, or deployed centrally via API. Same coach, any platform.

See it work
A student, the night before the deadline, asks:
Can you find two good articles for this and write my introduction?
Plain AI

Sure! Here are two sources and an introduction you can use:

Chen & Roberts (2019). Trust and accountability in project teams. Journal of Team Dynamics, 14(2), 88–104.not found

“This report analyses how communication failures shaped the team's decisions under pressure...”

wrote the graded work for you invented a source that doesn't exist generic – could be anyone's
With the coach

I won't find the articles or write the intro – those are the parts you're marked on. But I'll get you moving faster than a blank page:

What's the one management decision your analysis is really about? Tell me that, and we'll turn it into a searchable question and a sharper opening – in your words.

the marks stay yours – you write it real sources you can defend builds the skill, not the shortcut

Same request. One AI does the work for you – the other makes sure you can still do it yourself.

Click – ask plain AI
Beyond the gate
It doesn't just say no – it makes your thinking sharper.
“The main problem was poor communication, so I'll analyse it as a Planning problem.”
makes it specific
“Poor communication” is too broad for a strong analysis. How exactly did it create a planning problem – was critical information not shared before a decision was made?
“People were selfish and didn't share what they knew.”
separates fact from motive
Careful with “selfish” – that's a claim about motives you can't evidence. A defensible line: members held unique information that wasn't pooled before the decision. Which moment was that?

Two turns in, a vague complaint became a specific, defensible management claim – reasoned and written by the student.

And it holds
Pushed hard, it doesn't break.
Red-teamed through 8+ attack patterns:
write my intro just one topic sentence translate my draft give me the full outline which theory should I pick? fix my APA reference

Every hard boundary held – even under deadline pressure. The coaching got better, not weaker.

Why it matters
We're not policing AI.
We're building judgement.

As AI gets better at the writing and the analysis, the scarce human capability is judgement – deciding what matters, weighing the ethics, making the call. That's the core of management.

Deciding what matters
Weighing the ethics
Making the call

And it stays the student's choice – a habit can't be forced. But when the guided path is also the genuinely useful one, good habits form on their own: quietly, in the work, without resentment.

Human-centred education – with AI as the pressure, not the shortcut.
